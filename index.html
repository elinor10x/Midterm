Redesigning Montefiore Square

A greener, more comfortable, and more culturally active public space that improves shade, stormwater management, and everyday community use in Hamilton Heights.

See the redesign Read the proposal
Overview

Why this redesign works

My redesign improves Montefiore Square by combining environmental upgrades with cultural programming. I added more shade, permeable paving, planted areas, flexible seating, and a solar-powered digital art screen. Together, these interventions make the plaza cooler, more sustainable, more welcoming, and more reflective of the neighborhood around it.

Problem section

What problems does the plaza have now?

Lack of shade and heat exposure

Montefiore Square currently has limited shade, especially during warmer months when the space should be most useful. Although trees were planted in the recent renovation, they are still young and do not yet provide enough canopy. This makes parts of the plaza feel hot, exposed, and uncomfortable for daily use.

Stormwater runoff

Much of the plaza is made of hard, impervious surfaces that do not absorb rainwater. Instead, water runs off into the stormwater system, which contributes to flooding and environmental stress. A redesign should help the site manage water more naturally and sustainably.

Missing cultural identity

Even though the surrounding neighborhood has a strong artistic and historical identity, the plaza itself does not reflect that character. There is no permanent public art, interactive feature, or community storytelling element that connects the space to local culture and history.

Limited reasons to stay

The site works mainly as a pass-through space instead of a destination. It needs more seating, more visual interest, and more flexible uses so people feel encouraged to sit, gather, rest, and participate in community life.

Site analysis

Understanding the location

Montefiore Square is a triangular plaza located at Broadway, West 138th Street, and Hamilton Place in Hamilton Heights. It sits near the 137th Street–City College subway stop and is surrounded by a dense residential and commercial neighborhood. Because of its shape, the plaza has different edges and different pedestrian flows, which makes it an interesting site for redesign.

The surrounding community has a strong cultural identity and a rich history connected to Harlem, public art, education, and migration. This means the plaza should not only function as a green public space, but also reflect the character of the people who live and move through it every day.

In my redesign, I treated the site as both an environmental and social space. I focused on how people move through it, where shade is needed most, and where a cultural feature like a digital display could create the strongest visual and community impact.

Key features

Small interventions with a big impact

More canopy and shade

I added more mature, shade-providing trees and planting zones to reduce heat, improve comfort, and make the plaza more pleasant during warmer seasons.

Permeable paving

Replacing sections of hardscape with permeable paving allows rainwater to move through the ground more naturally, reducing runoff and improving environmental performance.

Community art screen

A solar-powered digital display creates a space for public art, local history, event announcements, and community storytelling.

Flexible social seating

Covered seating, vendor-friendly areas, and moveable furniture make the plaza more adaptable for resting, gathering, and small-scale local activity.

Featured design idea

The solar-powered digital art screen

The most distinctive part of my redesign is the solar-powered digital art screen. This feature would display rotating work by local artists, neighborhood history, cultural storytelling, and information about events happening nearby. Instead of treating the plaza only as a place to pass through, this element would make it a place to stop, look, and engage.

I chose this idea because it connects technology, sustainability, and culture in one feature. The screen would be powered by solar energy, which supports the environmental goals of the redesign while also making the installation feel innovative and appropriate for an AI and design-focused class project.

This idea also fits the neighborhood. Hamilton Heights and Harlem have deep artistic and cultural traditions, so the display could highlight local artists, school partnerships, historical content, and community submissions. In a real project, this feature could be managed through a partnership with community organizations, local schools, or arts groups that help select and rotate the content.

Design options

Alternatives I considered

Option A: Environment first

This option focused mostly on planting, shade, and stormwater management. It would improve comfort and sustainability, but it would not do enough to create a strong visual or cultural identity for the plaza.

Option B: Culture first

This option focused more heavily on programming and digital features, with a larger display and more event infrastructure. While it would make the plaza more active, it would not address environmental issues as strongly.

Option C: Integrated design

I selected this option because it combines the strongest parts of both approaches. It improves shade, planting, and paving while also adding the digital display and flexible seating. This balance makes the redesign more realistic and more complete.

AI reflection

How I used AI in this project

I used AI tools to help me visualize design ideas and develop the presentation of my proposal. AI was useful in quickly generating an early concept image and helping me think through different possible directions for the redesign.

At the same time, AI did not make the important design decisions for me. Some generated ideas were too generic and did not reflect the specific identity of Montefiore Square or the neighborhood around it. I had to decide which ideas were meaningful and which ones did not fit the site.

My own judgment was especially important in choosing to focus on the solar-powered digital art screen, connecting the redesign to local culture, and combining social, environmental, and technological elements into one proposal. AI helped me start the process, but I shaped the final direction of the project.

Revisions

How I improved the project

In revising my project, I focused on expanding the parts of the proposal that were too brief in the original version. I added a full problem section, a site analysis, design options, an AI reflection, and a budget so the project would feel more complete and realistic. I also developed my strongest idea—the solar-powered digital art screen—in much more detail by explaining its purpose, function, and relevance to the community. These revisions helped me turn my original concept into a stronger, more thoughtful website that better meets the assignment requirements.
